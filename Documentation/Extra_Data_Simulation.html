Why simulating data?

Data simulation is a useful tool when we are working with models, and it presents certain advantages over using real-world data. The main one is that if we know the data generating process, we know what results we should expect, and thus how well our models are fitting our data. When simulating data we decide parameter values, allowing us to compare model estimates to the real values we set. Simulating data often also helps us better understand the model, and how the data that we see can be a result of it.

Choosing what the data will look like

These three days we’ll be using an example dataset that I’ve generated specifically for this workshop. Each analysis will be looking at a different aspect of Step Selection Functions, but all we be conducted on the same dataset generated here. For this workshop I’ve chosen the Pyrenean chamois Rupicapra pyrenaica as the example species, simulating data as if we had tagged 10 individuals with GPS collars in the Catalan Pyrenees.

During the practical sessions, we will be analysing how chamois select different habitats available to them, and even how they are selecting the way they move itself. We will also introduce how we can allow selection to vary through time (in this case at a daily scale), and how to fit models when selection or movement behave non-linearly. To do so we will fit a set of models of increasing complexity to our data, always trying to capture the processes behind movement and selection as accurately as possible.

When simulating our datasets we will have to think about the entire movement and selection process. We will first define how our animals move in the absence of selection, and then add the selection component. We will also have to take into account our simulated sampling process (what GPS-fix intervals do we want, should we add location error, etc.)

Defining the sampling process

I have decided to keep things simple and simulating tracks for 10 animals which will be approximately 1000 locations each, all sampled at roughly 10min intervals. These are fairly realistic settings for data coming from larger terrestrial animals, and should work fine in our practical sessions. This doesn’t mean that SSFs can’t be done with data of less resolution or with fewer locations. Our study area will be the Cadí mountain range in northern Catalonia, where our simulated chamois will be moving. Tracks will be sampled throughout the day, and so will reflect movement and habitat selection for the entire sampling process. If we were interested in diurnal habitat selection we could simply discard nighttime locations and fit our models to the daytime locations only.

Defining the baseline movement kernel

We will be modelling movement in the traditional way: as a series of steps in two dimensions, characterized by a step length and a turning angle. This is the most common approach and works particularly well with Step Selection Functions, and many other state of the art methods1. It is simple and intuitive, and most current methods have been designed with it in mind. This doesn’t mean, however, that different movement kernels2 can’t be utilized in SSF. As we’ll see in the last day, you can get as creative as you want when it comes to customizing your movement kernel!

In this case we need to decide what kind of step length and turning angle distributions we want our animals to follow. For simplicity’s sake we will use an exponential distribution3 for step lengths and a Von Mises distribution4 for turning angles. These are two of the simplest distributions that fit movement data well, and can both be characterized by a single parameter. For the step length distribution we will characterize the rate parameter \(\lambda\) which relates directly to the mean of the distribution (which is \(1/\lambda\)). For the turning angle distribution we will characterize the concentration parameter \(\kappa\), which indicates how broad or narrow our turn-angle distribution will be.

I will now choose a value for rate and concentration that I think represents more or less what chamois will do in real life. This is probably way off what they really do, but for learning purposes let’s just roll with it. Since our sampling interval is 10 min, I will assume that on average chamois will move on average about 25m in that time. I will also assume that they tend to move in a straight line, but I don’t want to restrict movement too much. Since the rate of the exponential step length distribution is the inverse of the mean I will set \(\lambda = 1/25\). An intermediate value for the concentration parameter could be something like \(\kappa = 1\). Keep in mind that these values reflect what our chamois would do when there is no habitat selection involved, it’s sort of a baseline way of moving when the landscape is uniform. These also don’t mean that the chamois always takes steps with length \(25\) and turning angles of \(0º\), it just tells us how likely it is it will take a step of any given step length and turning angle.

Defining the selection process

Our chamois aren’t walking aimlessly across the landscape. They have areas that they like and areas they don’t like. They will tend to move away from the latter to search out the former. In this section I’m going to characterize how this preference manifests in our movement tracks. I will add habitat selection at two different levels: a difference in preference for certain habitat types (between open, shrubland and forest) and a preference for certain elevations.

Selection for different habitat types

We will be using a modified version of the Mapa de Cobèrtes del Sòl de Catalunya, in which we have pooled our habitat types into one of the following three:

  • Open: any open habitat such as grasslands, rocky terrain or constructed surface.

  • Shrubland: areas dominated by woody vegetation of low to medium height, with sparse or no tree cover.

  • Forest: any woodland with moderate to dense tree cover.

I will assume that our imaginary chamois will generally prefer open landscapes compared to shrubland and forest. Open landscapes are easier to move through and provide good foraging grounds, while shrubland and forest are harder to navigate and have less food availability5. I will encode this preference as the relative preference of one habitat type over the other. This means that one of my three habitat types (doesn’t matter which), will act as my reference value. We call this preference Relative Selection Strength (RSS). The RSS will tell us how much more likely it is that the chamois will choose the other habitat type over the reference habitat type. In this case if we set forest as out preference type we will have two RSS parameters:

  1. Preference for shrubland over forest (\(RSS_{shrubland}\))

  2. Preference for open over forest (\(RSS_{open}\))

The RSS parameters are typically treated on the log-odds scale (as log-RSS). What this means is that the probability of choosing the habitat type in question is \(exp(logRSS)\) times greater than the probability of choosing the reference habitat type. This means that:

  • \(logRSS > 0\): the habitat in question is more likely to be chosen than the reference.

  • \(logRSS = 0\): the habitat in question and the reference are as likely to be chosen.

  • \(logRSS < 0\): the habitat in question is less likely to be chosen than the reference.

Now I’m going to set values for the RSS of our habitats that reflect the chamois’ preference for open habitats over shrubland and forest.

# Setting log-RSS
logrss_shrubland <- 1.5 # Moderate selection for shrubland
logrss_open <- 4 # Strong selection for open habitats

Selection for altitude

Like many species, chamois have an optimal altitudinal range. This means that there is a minimum and maximum altitude at which they can be found, with an optimum somewhere in between. These relationships are rarely linear, and they often don’t follow any of the more common distributions available to us. In such cases we often work with models that allow fitting of non-linear relationships such as GAMs, which we will do on the third day of the workshop.

To simulate such a non-linear relationship I will overlap several normal distributions and smooth them using a spline function. This is just a way of achieving “messy-looking” non-linear data.

Code 
set.seed(123)

## Create altitude sequence and set base parameters ####
alt <- seq(0, 3500, length.out = 300)
# Set a primary and secondary peaks
peak1 <- 2250
peak2 <- 1700
peak3 <- 1950
peak4 <- 2300
peak5 <- 1600
peak6 <- 2600

## Add normal distributions at the peaks ####
# By overlapping several different normal distributions we get a "non-linear looking" response
alt_df <- data.frame(alt,
                     p1 = 1.5*exp(-0.5*((alt - peak1)/200)^2),
                     p2 = 0.4*exp(-0.5*((alt - peak2)/150)^2),
                     p3 = 0.5*exp(-0.5*((alt - peak3)/200)^2),
                     p4 = 0.1*exp(-0.5*((alt - peak4)/50)^2),
                     p5 = 0.1*exp(-0.5*((alt - peak5)/70)^2),
                     p6 = 1.2*exp(-0.5*((alt - peak6)/200)^2))
alt_df$pref <- alt_df$p1 + 
  alt_df$p2 + 
  alt_df$p3 + 
  alt_df$p4 + 
  alt_df$p5 + 
  alt_df$p6

## Fit it with a spline and plot #### 
spline_fit <- smooth.spline(alt_df$alt, alt_df$pref, spar = 0.6)
pref_smooth <- predict(spline_fit, alt_df$alt)$y
plot(alt_df$alt, pref_smooth, type = "l", lwd = 2, col = "blue",
     xlab = "Altitude (m)", ylab = "Log-RSS")

With continuous variables Relative Selection Strength becomes a bit harder to interpret. The value of logRSS for a certain altitude means little by itself, it only becomes meaningful when compared to a second point along the curve. For instance, in the example above: if the Log-RSS value at 2000m is approximately 1.2 and the Log-RSS value at 2500m is approximately 1.5, the animal’s preference for 2000m compared to 2500m is equal to -0.3 on the log-odds scale. When transforming back to the RSS scale 6 we get that the probability of selecting a location at 2000m is \(exp(-0.3) = 0.74\) times the probability of selecting a location at 2500m (all else being equal).

Adding time to the movement kernel

It’s reasonable to expect the movement kernel (i.e. the step length and turning angle distributions) to change throughout the day. Our animals are probably going to be less active during the night and more active during the day. A typical way of simulating and modelling temporal effects is using sine-cosine curves. If we add a term to our model where the response depends on \(sin(time) + cos(time)\)7, the response will fluctuate throughout the day, with a maximum and a minimum.

To do so we can simply modify the parameter values of the step length (the rate of the exponential distribution) and the turning angle distribution (the concentration of the Von Mises distribution). The easiest way is to multiply them by the sin & cos terms as follows.

Simulating the tracks

Now that we have defined how our animals will move it’s time to run the actual simulations. To do so we will need the following:

  • A habitat map: I have prepared it, and put it in the Data/Cartography folder

  • An elevation map: same as the habitat map

  • Initial locations for our animals: I have decided ten points at random close to the center of our habitat map which will serve as our starting points.

  • Some extra settings: which I will determine a bit further down (e.g. how long the tracks will be, adding a bit of sampling noise, etc)

  • The parameters I set in the previous section: the movement and selection kernels

To simulate our trajectories I will do something similar to the process of fitting an SSF. I will take a location, generate several random steps from this location using the movement kernel, and choose one using the selection kernel. Usually the more likely step will be chosen, but every now and then an unlikely location (e.g. at a weird habitat and altitude) will be picked, like would happen in any process involving stochasticity. After this we take the end of the step we just simulated as our new starting point and start over. Overall the pattern that will emerge after simulating enough steps should follow the distributions that we specified and look somewhat like a track that an animal would actually produce.

Simulating steps and selection

Simulating the track is now just a matter of applying the rules I set earlier to our step-generating and choosing process. We take our movement kernel, with the parameters specified earlier, generate random steps from it and choose one based on the probabilities defined by the selection kernel. I will do this by writing a function that does the entire process for a chosen number of steps.

  1. Starting with the initial location, generate 10 random steps from the step length and turning angle distributions

  2. Extract altitude and habitat for each location

  3. Assign selection odds for each location and choose one step based on these

  4. Repeat points 1 to 3 starting with the last selected step

Code 
## Writing a track simulation function ####
tracksim <- function(initloc,
                     inittime,
                     n_steps,
                     step_interval,
                     n_candidates,
                     sl_lambda,
                     ta_kappa,
                     alt_raster, 
                     hab_raster,
                     alt_selection,
                     hab_selection,
                     beta_lambda1,
                     beta_lambda2,
                     beta_kappa1,
                     beta_kappa2){
  # Storage for simulated track
  track <- matrix(NA, nrow = n_steps + 1, ncol = 2)
  track[1, ] <- initloc[[1]]
  
  # Storage for times
  times <- rep(as.POSIXct(NA), n_steps + 1)
  times[1] <- inittime[[1]]
  
  # Initialize previous angle (random start)
  prev_angle <- runif(1, 0, 2*pi)
  
  # Get study area bounds once
  raster_ext <- ext(alt_raster)
  xmin <- raster_ext[1]
  xmax <- raster_ext[2]
  ymin <- raster_ext[3]
  ymax <- raster_ext[4]
  
  # Pre-compute time-related constants
  two_pi_over_24 <- 2 * pi / 24
  
  # Simulate steps
  for (i in 1:n_steps) {
    
    # Current time in fractional hours
    time_hour <- as.numeric(difftime(times[i], 
                                     as.POSIXct(format(times[i], 
                                                       "%Y-%m-%d 00:00:00")), 
                                     units = "hours"))
    
    # Adjust lambda and kappa for time of day
    time_rad <- time_hour * two_pi_over_24
    lambda_time <- sl_lambda + beta_lambda1 * sin(time_rad) + 
      beta_lambda2 * cos(time_rad)
    kappa_time  <- ta_kappa + beta_kappa1 * sin(time_rad) + 
      beta_kappa2 * cos(time_rad)
    
    # Generate candidates until all are within bounds
    repeat {
      sl_candidates <- rexp(n_candidates, rate = lambda_time)
      ta_candidates <- rvonmises(n_candidates,
                                mu = circular(prev_angle),
                                kappa = kappa_time)
    
      # Vectorized candidate computation
      x_new <- track[i,1] + sl_candidates * cos(ta_candidates)
      y_new <- track[i,2] + sl_candidates * sin(ta_candidates)
      
      # Check bounds
      inside <- x_new >= xmin & x_new <= xmax & y_new >= ymin & y_new <= ymax
    
      if (all(inside)) {
        candidates <- cbind(x_new, y_new)
        break
      }
    }
    
    # Extract altitude and habitat for each candidate (in one call each)
    alt_extracted <- terra::extract(alt_raster, candidates)
    hab_extracted <- terra::extract(hab_raster, candidates)
    
    alt_values <- as.numeric(alt_extracted[, ncol(alt_extracted)])
    hab_values <- as.numeric(hab_extracted[, ncol(hab_extracted)])
    
    # Compute selection odds
    alt_odds <- exp(predict(alt_selection, alt_values)$y)  
    hab_odds <- exp(hab_selection[hab_values])  
    total_odds <- alt_odds * hab_odds
    
    # Sample one step based on selection odds
    chosen_idx <- sample.int(n_candidates, size = 1, prob = total_odds)
    
    # Save chosen location
    track[i+1, ] <- candidates[chosen_idx, ]
    
    # Update time (pre-compute step_interval * 60)
    times[i+1] <- times[i] + step_interval * 60
    
    # Update previous angle
    prev_angle <- atan2(candidates[chosen_idx,2] - track[i,2],
                        candidates[chosen_idx,1] - track[i,1])
  }
  
  return(data.frame(x = track[,1], y = track[,2], time = times))
}

Adding some “noise” to the data to make it look more realistic

Now we just add some jitter to our positions and timestamps to make the track look a bit more realistic. We also can delete a few locations so that during the practice session we will see how to deal with missing data. I am writing a function to handle the resampling that takes the tracks generated before and adds:

  • Some variation in fix intervals, since GPS don’t always capture locations in perfect intervals.

  • Small location error, reflecting current GPS standards

  • Some gaps in the data, for moments where satellite coverage was insufficient or some other technical malfunctions, so we can see how to handle gaps in the data

Code 
set.seed(123)

## Writing a function to create some noise ####
noise_track <- function(track,
                        sd_time,
                        sd_space,
                        p_drop) {     
  
  n <- nrow(track)
  
  ## a) Jitter time 
  time_jitter <- rnorm(n, mean = 0, sd = sd_time)
  track$time <- track$time + time_jitter
  
  ## b) Jitter space (bivariate normal, independent x/y)
  track$x <- track$x + rnorm(n, mean = 0, sd = sd_space)
  track$y <- track$y + rnorm(n, mean = 0, sd = sd_space)
  
  ## c) Randomly delete locations
  keep <- runif(n) > p_drop
  track <- track[keep, ]
  
  rownames(track) <- NULL
  track
}

## Running the function to obtain the final track ####

  4. The Von Mises distribution is similar to a normal distribution in a circular space. In movement ecology we tend to assume that \(\mu = 0\), leaving us with only \(\kappa\) as the parameter to estimate.↩︎
